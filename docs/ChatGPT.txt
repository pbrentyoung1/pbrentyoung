Working Title
The Right Story, Told the Right Way
Every organization is telling a story. The question is whether it's the one you intended.
Big Idea: Great communication isn't about saying more. It's about intentionally shaping the story people experience.

The Question
Have you ever visited a church that seemed to have everything?

The music was excellent. The preaching was thoughtful. The people were welcoming.

And yet somehow...it all felt disconnected.

Or maybe you've had the opposite experience.

The logo wasn't particularly memorable. The website was simple. The building showed its age.

But everything felt unmistakably authentic.

You knew who they were within five minutes.

Why?

What makes one organization feel clear while another feels confusing?

The Problem
Most organizations think communication happens when they speak.

It doesn't.

Communication begins long before anyone steps onto a stage or posts on social media.

It begins with the parking lot.

The signage.

The website.

The volunteer who greets you at the door.

The photography.

The typography.

The follow-up email.

The language on the giving page.

Every interaction quietly answers the same question:

"Who are we?"

The problem isn't that organizations don't communicate.

It's that they're communicating unintentionally.

Every organization tells a story.

Not every organization chooses it.

The Stakes
When the story is unclear, people work harder than they should have to.

Visitors wonder if they belong.

Volunteers guess what success looks like.

Creative teams argue over preferences instead of purpose.

Leaders confuse activity with clarity.

Eventually the mission doesn't become less important.

It simply becomes harder to recognize.

The Reframe
For years I thought I worked in design.

Then I thought I worked in marketing.

Eventually I realized neither was true.

I help organizations become intentional about the stories they're already telling.

That's a very different job.

A logo isn't the destination.

A website isn't the destination.

A sermon graphic isn't the destination.

They're evidence of something deeper.

Good communication isn't decoration.

It's alignment.

It's the moment when what you believe, what you say, and what people experience finally tell the same story.

The Guide
Over the last two decades I've had the privilege of working with churches, nonprofits, Fortune 500 companies, filmmakers, educators, trauma counselors, and ministry leaders.

The projects looked completely different.

The questions were always the same.

Who are we trying to reach?

What do they need to understand?

What obstacles stand in the way?

What story are they experiencing today?

What story should they experience tomorrow?

Those questions have shaped every brand I've helped build, every website I've redesigned, every film I've produced, every communication system I've developed, and eventually the software I'm building today.

The tools keep changing.

The questions don't.
