There is a question sitting underneath every ad, every website, every product launch, and every sermon series. Marketers spend billions chasing it. Churches quietly wonder about it. It is this: how do people actually change?

Long before anyone drew a funnel on a whiteboard, a tired traveler sat down beside a well, in the middle of a place he was not supposed to be, and started a conversation with a person nobody else would have chosen. By the end of the afternoon, he had answered the question completely.

This is the first piece in a series about what that well can teach us. John gives us one long, patient scene in chapter four, the woman at the well, and it turns out to be a nearly complete picture of how a person moves from stranger to seeker to believer to someone running back to town saying "come and see."

## Building a well and waiting

Churches tend to think about this in one of two broken ways. Some treat outreach like a building project: put up a beautiful well, open the doors, and wait for the thirsty to find us. Others hear the word "marketing" and flinch, because so much of it really is manipulation in a nicer outfit, and they want no part of it. Both instincts are trying to protect something good. Both also leave people stranded on the far side of a gap that nobody is helping them cross.

Jesus did neither. He did not build a well and wait. He went through Samaria, the long way, the wrong side of town. And when he got there, he did not treat the woman he met as a demographic to be converted. He treated her as a person to be known. What happened next was not a funnel. It was a journey toward transformation.

## The shape of the journey

That journey has a shape, and this series will follow it one step at a time. He goes to the marketplace, where people already are. He meets a specific person, not a category. He opens with a small, human first touchpoint, a request for a drink. He listens for the thirst underneath the thirst. He offers a future worth wanting. He earns the right to tell the truth. He lets the conversation rise from her question to the real one. Her life visibly changes, and she leaves her water jar behind. She runs to tell the town, and the town comes to see for themselves.

Marketplace, person, touchpoint, pain, promise, trust, truth, transformation, advocacy, community. That is the path. If you have ever built a communications plan, it is a strangely familiar one.

## What this is, and what it is not

Let me be clear about what this series is not. It is not an attempt to baptize marketing tactics by stapling a verse to them. It is the opposite claim: that the best things communication has ever learned about empathy, clarity, trust, and invitation are dim echoes of something Jesus understood perfectly. People move toward change through relationship, meaning, trust, and truth. We did not invent that. At our best, we are only remembering it.

And it comes with guardrails we will hold the whole way. The Holy Spirit changes people; communication never manufactures a conversion. People are never leads to be captured. They are neighbors to be loved. A persona is a tool for empathy, not a stereotype. Pain is something to understand, never to exploit. We earn the ask instead of demanding it. Every step bends toward dignity, because the moment it stops, it stops being ministry.

So consider this an invitation to walk back to Samaria together. Over the next several pieces we will stand at each part of the well and ask what it means for the way your church actually shows up: online, in the parking lot, in the follow-up email nobody thought mattered. Not so you can grow a number. So you can love the real people God is already sending toward you, at every step of the road, and not only in the moment they finally walk in.

## At the well

Here is the question to carry into the rest of the series. If someone near you is quietly thirsty right now, out in the marketplace where they already live, would anything you are doing help them take the next honest step? Or are you building a beautiful well, and waiting?
