The first season of Seinfeld is bad. Not rough-around-the-edges bad. Actually bad. Five episodes, one of the shortest first seasons in television history, and almost none of the show you remember.

Elaine is not there yet. She is not in the pilot at all. She only arrives because the network asks for a female character. Kramer is not Kramer either. In the pilot he is a shy shut-in named Kessler who does not like to leave his apartment, the opposite of the man who will later explode through Jerry's door. And George is a Woody Allen impression. Jason Alexander has said he first played him as a nervous, stammering intellectual, before he understood that George was really Larry David.

Here is the part worth stealing. Seinfeld did not become the best sitcom ever made by polishing that first season. Nobody tightened the lighting or punched up the jokes on the same tired setup. They changed the thing itself. They added Elaine. They rebuilt Kramer. They let George become George. They dropped the stand-up crutch and let the stories tangle together. The fix was not craft. The fix was change.

Most churches, when something is not working, reach for the opposite. New logo, new website, new lower thirds. Six months and a decent budget later, everything is undeniably nicer, and nothing is different. The instinct is almost always to polish. The honest answer is often to change.

## Two kinds of problems

There are only two kinds of creative problems, and almost every frustrating meeting comes from confusing them.

A **polish problem** means the thing is right but rough: correct message, right audience, weak execution. A **change problem** means the thing itself is wrong: the strategy, the assumption, the offering. Polish problems are solved with craft. Change problems are solved with decisions. Each one is completely immune to the other's medicine.

> ! Craft can't fix a decision. Decisions can't fix craft.

## Polish amplifies, it does not correct

This is why you can't polish your way into relevance. Polish makes true things beautiful. It can't make wrong things true. If your announcements are unclear, better typography gives you *beautifully* unclear announcements. If your church is genuinely built for a community that no longer lives in your zip code, a rebrand just hands that mismatch a nicer wardrobe. Craft is an amplifier. It makes whatever signal you feed it louder and cleaner. Feed it the wrong signal and you have paid good money to be wrong more legibly.

> ! A rebrand is a louder microphone. It is not a better sermon.

## Misdiagnosis runs both ways

Apply polish to a change problem and you get the church that redesigns its ministry brochure every year while the ministry it describes quietly stopped working in 2019. The brochure gets better. The problem stays fed and warm underneath it.

Apply change to a polish problem and you get the opposite failure: the team that scraps a working strategy because the last two executions were sloppy. That is amputating a limb that needed a splint. Strategy takes the blame for what craft should have caught.

## The one question to ask first

When something is not working, ask one question before anything else: *if this exact idea were executed flawlessly, would it work?*

If yes (the sermon series is right but the graphics are muddy, the event is right but the signage failed), you have a polish problem. Hand it to craft, and protect the strategy from the room's panic.

If no (flawless execution would still land on people who are not listening, or would say something that is no longer true), you have a change problem. No designer can help you yet. It has to go back to leadership, and going back is the win, not the failure.

The change questions are the uncomfortable ones, which is exactly why polish is so tempting. Nobody's identity is threatened by a font. But relevance comes from riskier questions. Who are we for? Are they actually here? Does what we do Monday through Saturday back up what we say on Sunday? Would the people we claim to reach recognize themselves anywhere in our building or our feed? No amount of kerning answers those.

## Why this is a leadership skill

Teams under pressure default to polish, because polish is visible effort and change is visible risk. Somebody has to be willing to say "this isn't a design problem" in a room that would rather order another revision. That somebody is a leader, whatever their title says.

Get the story right, then tell it right. Polish applied to a clarified mission is worth every hour it takes. Polish applied *instead of* clarity is just an expensive way to postpone the conversation. Seinfeld got that order right. They fixed the show first. Then they made it shine.
