Nobody gets excited about a creative brief. It's a form. It asks unglamorous questions: who is this for, what's the one job, when does it ship, who approves it. In a culture that prizes spontaneity as a sign of the Spirit, a brief can feel almost faithless.

I want to argue the opposite: the creative brief is one of the most pastoral documents in your communications department.

## A brief is care for the audience

Every question on a brief is really the same question: *have we actually thought about the person on the receiving end?* Who are they? What do they already know, fear, assume? What do we want for them (not just from them)? A church that can't fill out a brief isn't being free-spirited. It's confessing that it hasn't thought about the people it's called to serve.

Writing it down is the moment vague goodwill becomes actual intention.

## A brief is care for the team

An unclear assignment doesn't stay unclear. It converts into anxiety, and the anxiety lands on the least powerful person in the process. The volunteer designer redoing a slide for the fourth time isn't suffering for the gospel; she's suffering for a missing paragraph. A brief moves the hard conversation to the *beginning*, where it costs an hour, instead of the end, where it costs a person.

> ! Clarity is kindness. Ambiguity always rolls downhill until it lands on a volunteer.

## A brief is care for the message

Unbriefed work gets judged by preference (whoever's in the room, whatever mood they're in). Briefed work gets judged by intent: did this do the job we agreed on, for the people we named? The brief is what lets craft serve the mission instead of the meeting. (That is the referee at work, which I unpack in [Brand Is the Referee](/blog/brand-is-the-referee).)

## What actually goes in a brief

So what belongs in one? A brief does not need to be long, it needs to be honest. It names the audience. It names the one job, the single thing this piece has to do, and the single action you are hoping for. It says what that person needs to know, feel, and how they should respond. It sets a real deadline and names who signs off, so approval becomes a step instead of a surprise. And it sets a budget (time as well as money). Those few honest answers are the difference between a team guessing and a team aiming.

## Every brief has a budget

Here is where people push back. We have no money for this one. Fine. You still have a budget, because you still have time, and time is the currency church communications actually spends. So budget the hours, not just the dollars. Guess how long the writing, the design, the revisions, and the sign-offs will take, and write the guess down.

The point is not to be right. The point is to have something to learn from. When the guess is wildly off, the gap becomes a question worth asking. Did the work take longer than I thought? Did I underestimate one part of the deliverable? Did approvals stall for a week? Did we quietly change the project halfway through? A time budget turns a painful overrun into information you can use next time. Without one, every project just feels hard, and you never find out why.

## Where a brief lives

A brief also has an altitude. The creative brief belongs to the campaign, where it answers the big questions for the whole season: who we are trying to reach, what we want for them, and what winning looks like. From there, the project is the plan to carry that brief out, and the plan feeds the deliverables, which feed the tasks. So you are not filling out paperwork for every little thing. You are writing the north star down once, at the top, so every deliverable underneath has something true to be measured against. (If campaign, project, deliverable, and task feel fuzzy, I sorted them out in [Stop Calling Everything a Project](/blog/stop-calling-everything-a-project).)

So no, the brief isn't bureaucracy sneaking into the church. It's the same impulse behind sermon prep and worship rehearsal: love for people, expressed as preparation. We'd never say "the Spirit will provide" as a reason to skip rehearsing the band. Stop saying it about the communication.
